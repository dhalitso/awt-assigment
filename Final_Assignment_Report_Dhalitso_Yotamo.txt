1. Abstract
This report presents Nopin, an innovative, mobile-first real estate ecosystem engineered to address the highly fragmented and inefficient property market in Mozambique. Traditional digital real estate systems often introduce significant visual and cognitive load by relying heavily on interactive map overlays crowded with hundreds of overlapping, unverified location pins, leading to "map fatigue" and user frustration.

Using a strict User-Centered Design (UCD) approach, Nopin introduces an intentional dual-application architecture that separates concerns into a Buyer App (focused on direct, search-driven discovery) and a Seller App (focused on credit-based listing management, identity verification, and lead analytics). This division ensures that the visual interface matches the distinct mental models of each user class. By applying core Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) theories — such as Jakob Nielsen's Usability Heuristics, Don Norman's Design Principles, Fitts's Law, and Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory — the platform streamlines real estate workflows.

This document traces the system's development from user research and low-fidelity sketches to a production-ready front-end implementation deployed on Vercel, demonstrating how human-centered design improves data legibility, reduces user error rates, and enhances overall trust in digital transactions.

2. Domain Context, Problem Statement & Motivation

2.1 Domain Context
The real estate and housing sector in Mozambican urban centers like Maputo, Matola, and Beira has seen rapid expansion. However, the commercial infrastructure supporting property discovery and transaction remains heavily informal, slow-moving, and dependent on human intermediaries. Finding a house to rent or buy is a manual task that takes months, while listing a property is expensive due to broker fees.

2.2 Problem Statement: Research Case Studies
The friction in the current Mozambican property market is characterized by severe information asymmetry, lack of data accuracy, and transaction insecurity. Two real-world case studies gathered during the user research phase define the core problems:

* Case 1 — Interview with Tomás (31, civil engineer seeking to rent in Maputo):
  Tomás spent over 8 months searching for a house. He was entirely dependent on informal real estate brokers (bulaqueiros) who controlled what listings he saw. They frequently sent properties that did not match his specifications in terms of size, price, or location. Tomás had no independent means to search, filter listings, or verify property status. He visited 11 properties in person, wasting time and transport costs, before settling for an inadequate house out of sheer exhaustion.
* Case 2 — Contextual Inquiry with Hélio (27, graphic designer searching for 2 months):
  Hélio searched for rental apartments using Facebook Marketplace and WhatsApp groups. He described the experience as chaotic, encountering duplicate listings, hidden pricing ("inbox me"), expired listings, and scams. He spent 30 to 45 minutes daily scrolling feeds, unable to filter by location or set persistent search alerts. He lost track of promising properties because they disappeared down the group chats.

2.3 Motivation & Cognitive Gaps
From an HCI perspective, the current informal real estate search ecosystem represents an interaction failure:
1. High Cognitive Load: Users must manually extract, compare, and verify unstructured data across WhatsApp, Facebook, phone logs, and physical visits, overloading short-term working memory.
2. No System Transparency & Feedback: Seekers have no visibility into listing status (whether a property is rented or still available) without making phone calls, leading to lost time.
3. Visual Clutter and Map Fatigue: Existing map-based platforms crowd small mobile viewports with dense, overlapping location pins. This visual noise violates Gestalt principles and makes target selection difficult.
4. Financial Friction: Sellers pay steep broker commissions (often equivalent to one month's rent) and must post listings repeatedly across multiple platforms without receiving performance analytics.

Nopin is motivated by the desire to replace this chaotic system with a clean, structured, card-driven dual-app interface that provides direct connection, real-time feedback, and verified listings while eliminating visual clutter.

3. Project Objectives

3.1 General Objective
To design, prototype, and implement a human-centered, dual-app mobile real estate platform tailored for the Mozambican market that lowers cognitive load, eliminates reliance on unverified middle-men, and replaces pin-heavy spatial layouts with an intuitive, component-driven interaction system.

3.2 Specific Objectives
1. Conduct field research and contextual inquiries to map the behavior and pain points of local property seekers and sellers in Mozambique.
2. Minimize spatial cognitive load by implementing progressive disclosure mechanics, replacing dense map-pin layers with high-contrast property cards.
3. Formulate a distinct dual-app architecture that maps directly to the separate mental models of buyers and sellers without causing context-switching confusion.
4. Implement an accessible, production-ready front-end web application hosted on Vercel to validate layout responsiveness and interactive state changes.
5. Establish a structured Usability Evaluation Plan incorporating expert heuristic reviews and targeted user tasks to measure success quantitatively.

4. User Research & Requirements Engineering

4.1 Research Methodology
Requirements elicitation used two primary HCI research methods:
1. Semi-Structured Interviews: Conducted with 5 participants (3 seekers, 2 property owners) to understand their real estate journeys, emotional pain points, search strategies, and financial friction.
2. Contextual Inquiries: Observed participants (including Tomás and Hélio) navigating Facebook groups, WhatsApp, and phone contacts in their natural settings to track search times, data loss, and context switching.

4.2 Target Users
* Buyers and Renters: Tech-literate, smartphone-active individuals (aged 20–55) in urban Mozambique looking to rent or purchase residential or commercial properties.
* Property Owners and Sellers: Individual owners and independent developers seeking a cost-effective, self-directed tool to list properties, manage visibility, and chat with buyers.

4.3 User Personas
To guide design decisions, the field data was synthesized into two representative archetypes:

* Persona 1: Tomás Sitoe (The Seeker - Buyer App):
  A 31-year-old Maputo professional looking for a 2-bedroom rental apartment in Sommerschield under 30,000 MT/month. He is tech-literate (uses Google Maps, WhatsApp, and banking apps) but frustrated by broker gatekeeping, lack of search filters, and spent 8 months chasing irrelevant leads.
* Persona 2: Paulo Santos (The Owner - Seller App):
  A 26-year-old property owner wishing to sell a plot of land in Matola. He wants to avoid paying a 10% commission to brokers and is tired of manually re-posting listings in Facebook groups daily with no feedback on audience reach.

4.4 System Requirements Derived from Research
* SR-1 (Direct Connection): Connect buyers and sellers directly via integrated messaging and calling, bypassing broker intermediaries.
* SR-2 (Granular Filtering): Implement search filters (price range, property type, location) to reduce cognitive clutter.
* SR-3 (Listing Verification): Introduce identity validation for sellers to reduce scam risks.
* SR-4 (Transparent Credit System): Replace commissions with a low-cost, credit-based daily visibility model for sellers.
* SR-5 (Dynamic Notifications): Inform buyers of listing availability changes in real-time.

5. Interaction Design Approach & HCI Foundations

5.1 Interaction Style: Graphical User Interface & Direct Manipulation
Nopin uses a mobile-first Graphical User Interface (GUI) emphasizing touch-based direct manipulation. Users interact with physical property cards, map targets, and attribute chips. Tapping, swiping, and scrolling produce immediate, visible, and reversible system feedback. This aligns with Shneiderman's Golden Rules by giving users a sense of control and reducing data entry errors.

5.2 Design Thinking Process
We followed the five-stage Design Thinking methodology (IDEO):
1. Empathise: Interviewed local users about search duration, costs, broker bias, and trust deficits.
2. Define: Framed the core design challenges: direct filtered discovery for seekers, and commission-free visible listing channels for owners.
3. Ideate: Conceived features including progressive filtering sheets, credit wallets, OTP logins, and map search toggle buttons.
4. Prototype: Built visual layouts, components, and transitions.
5. Test: Outlined heuristic assessments and task metrics (TCR, SUS).

5.3 HCI Theoretical Foundations
* Fitts's Law:
  T = a + b log2 ( 2D / W )
  Traditional platforms use tiny, overlapping map pins, increasing target acquisition time (T) and misclicks. Nopin replaces crowded pin arrays with large, swipeable property cards (increasing target width W), reducing movement time (T) and selection errors.
* Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory:
  To eliminate extraneous cognitive load, Nopin uses progressive disclosure. Advanced filters are tucked inside expandable bottom sheets, displaying only core details (price, location, type) on initial search, revealing complexity only on demand.
* Paivio's Dual-Coding Theory:
  To aid rapid recognition, property specifications (bedrooms, bathrooms, area) are always paired with vector icons alongside text (e.g., a Bed icon next to "2 Quartos"), leveraging visual and verbal cognitive pathways.
* Miller's Law (Short-Term Memory Reduction):
  Human working memory holds 7 ± 2 items. To prevent memory decay during negotiations, Nopin pins the relevant property listing card to the top of active chat screens, keeping transaction context visible without requiring recall.

5.4 Design Principles Applied
* Affordance & Signifiers (Norman): Tappable cards feature subtle drop shadows and borders, signaling their clickability. Color choices act as signifiers (e.g., green checkmarks indicate verified sellers).
* Feedback (Norman): Every interaction triggers immediate visual updates. Document scanning outlines show progress, and top-ups display payment states.
* Gestalt Principles:
  - Proximity: Enclosing photos, price, and specs inside a single bordered card groups them as a single property.
  - Similarity: Uniform listing cards allow users to scan and compare properties quickly.
  - Figure-Ground: Dimming the background map to 35% opacity when a filter modal is active draws focus to the configuration controls.

6. Low-Fidelity Wireframes & User Interaction Flow

6.1 User Flow Navigation Sequences
The application defines two main user flow sequences:
* Buyer Flow: [Onboarding/SSO] -> [Home/Filter Feed] -> [Select Card] -> [Map/Property details] -> [Chat/Call]
* Seller Flow: [Onboarding/OTP] -> [Identity Check Scan] -> [Create Listing Form] -> [Select Visibility] -> [M-Pesa Pay]

6.2 Wireframe Layout Descriptions
* Onboarding & Auth Layout: Sequential carousel slides with illustrative imagery, dynamic dot indicators, a skip button, and a central input form with password visibility toggles.
* Feed & Map Layout: A split screen showing search filter chips at the top, a card list below, and an "Explore Map" button that expands the map layout to 100% viewport width while hiding textual items.
* ID Verification Wizard Layout: A camera viewport container with a green laser animation overlay, document boundary framing guides, and simple "Yes/No" review options.
* Chat View Layout: A chat list header containing the contact name and dial button, a pinned property context card, a scrollable bubble area, and a bottom pill input block with a circular send action.

7. Design Justification & Jakob Nielsen's Heuristic Alignment
The Nopin system aligns with Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics as follows:

1. Visibility of System Status (Heuristic #1): Real-time status tags (Green: "Disponível", Grey: "Pendente", Red: "Vendida") are shown on listing cards. Upload progress loaders keep users informed during file submission.
2. Match Between System and the Real World (Heuristic #2): Localization uses local Mozambican currency formatting (MT / Meticais) and standard search terms (Arrendar, Comprar, Terrenos, Comercial).
3. User Control and Freedom (Heuristic #3): Modals have clear "X" close actions, and back arrows allow navigation out of deep views. The wallet checkout includes a confirmation step to cancel purchases before credits are deducted.
4. Consistency and Standards (Heuristic #4): Both apps share a unified color scheme, typographic scale, and UI components.
5. Error Prevention (Heuristic #5): Form submit buttons remain disabled until all required inputs pass validation. Phone number fields automatically strip invalid characters.
6. Recognition Rather Than Recall (Heuristic #6): Pinned listing cards in chat threads keep property details visible, removing the need for users to remember listing information.
7. Aesthetic and Minimalist Design (Heuristic #8): Clean, whitespace-friendly cards replace dense map overlays, reducing visual fatigue.

8. Technical Architecture & Feature Implementation

Nopin's front-end application is built using React, Vite, and vanilla CSS, and is deployed as a serverless-supported application on Vercel at awt-assigment.vercel.app.

8.1 Onboarding & Dynamic OTP Verification
To bypass external SMTP configuration failures, Nopin integrates directly with the EmailJS API to deliver verification codes:
* Upon login or sign-up, the application generates a random 6-digit OTP code.
* It sends a POST request to api.emailjs.com/api/v1.0/email/send, passing the template parameters dynamically. The client-side code packages the full verification message:  
  "Olá! O seu código de verificação temporário para o Nopin é: [code]"
* If EmailJS keys are missing from the environment variables, the system displays a fallback UI notification containing the OTP code for continuous testing.

8.2 Standardized Navigation & Dual-App Concerns
To match the high-fidelity designs, the main workspace navigation uses a tab bar with:
1. Explorar: Interactive property card feed and filters.
2. Mensagem: User-to-seller chat logs with active unread count badges.
3. Ofertas: Map-based listing discovery.
4. Favoritos: Saved listings panel.
5. Meu Perfil: User profile settings, verification status, and credit wallet.

8.3 Identity Verification & Biometric Wizard
To prevent fraud, Nopin includes a 9-step document and biometric ID verification wizard in Profile.jsx:
* Step 1-2: Introduction page and document type selection (ID, Passport, Driver License).
* Step 3-4: Document scanning camera simulation. It renders a dark scanner window, a green laser line animation, a mock ID layout, and a capture action, followed by a review screen.
* Step 5-7: Biometric selfie capture simulation. It shows a face oval guide and a scanning line, followed by a preview of the captured portrait.
* Step 8-9: Processing state with progress bars, culminating in the user profile being awarded the green verified badge.

8.4 Recharge Cart, Credit Economy & Logged-In Checkout Sync
To support the self-directed seller model, Profile.jsx includes a checkout flow for credit top-ups:
* Wallet Integration: Shows the current balance and predefined packages (Starter: 10 credits for 100 MT; Standard: 50 credits for 500 MT; Premium: 120 credits for 1000 MT) or custom entries (1 credit = 10 MT).
* Logged-In Checkout Sync: Displays a read-only E-mail de Faturação input pre-filled with user.email. This visually confirms that the purchase receipt is linked directly to the logged-in user's credentials.
* Checkout Validation: Strips spaces and validates various phone number formats (e.g. +258 84 123 4567, 258 84 123 4567, or 841234567), standardizing them to the required 258xxxxxxxx format.
* Email Receipt Delivery: Calls EmailJS to deliver a formatted billing receipt to user.email, detailing the transaction ID, quantity of credits, cost, date, and phone number.

8.5 Real M-Pesa Ecosystem STK Push Integration
To trigger actual transaction popups on mobile phones, the checkout sends payment requests to the serverless proxy route mpesa.js:
* Ecosystem Execution: The app removes mock in-app PIN pads. When the payment request starts, the app enters a waiting state ("Aguardando PIN no telemóvel..."), while the backend calls the M-Pesa sandbox API, triggering a real push notification to the user's phone.
* Session-ID Authentication: The proxy encrypts the developer API Key using RSA OAEP padding and the Vodacom public key, requests a Session ID from the auth endpoint, and initiates the C2B transaction using c2bPayment/singleStage/.
* Public Key PEM Normalization: To prevent Vercel deployment crashes caused by key formatting, the backend normalizes MPESA_PUBLIC_KEY. It replaces literal \n or \\n strings with newlines and wraps the key in standard PEM headers (-----BEGIN PUBLIC KEY----- / -----END PUBLIC KEY-----) if missing.
* Sandbox Fallback: If API keys are not set, the endpoint returns a simulated response explaining how to configure the keys on Vercel.

8.6 Supabase Visitor Analytics Engine
To capture platform performance, we built a visitor analytics hook useVisitorTracker.js:
* Data Collection: Captures the user-agent string (parsing it to identify browser and device details) and fetches ipapi.co to retrieve the client's city and country.
* Session Duration: Tracks active time by incrementing a counter every 10 seconds and writing the final duration to the database on page unload.
* Seeder Database Sync: Integrates with a Supabase PostgreSQL instance to log these session analytics.

9. Usability Evaluation Plan & Metrics

9.1 Heuristic Evaluation Framework
A panel of three HCI experts will perform a heuristic evaluation of the deployed application against Jakob Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics. Identified issues will be rated on a severity scale from 0 to 4:
* 0: Usability cosmetic problem only.
* 1: Minor usability problem (low priority).
* 2: Moderate usability problem (medium priority).
* 3: Major usability problem (high priority - must be fixed).
* 4: Usability catastrophe (imperative to fix before release).

9.2 User Testing Task Framework
Ten representative users will complete 5 tasks in the Buyer App and 5 tasks in the Seller App while using the Think Aloud Protocol:

- Task 01: Complete registration and sign in. | Verify account identity using the document scan wizard.
- Task 02: Search for a T2 apartment under 30,000 MT. | Publish a new property listing with pictures and specs.
- Task 03: Save a target listing to your Favourites list. | Set a listing to Premium and calculate credits cost.
- Task 04: Chat with a property owner directly. | Check wallet credit balance and top up via M-Pesa.
- Task 05: Navigate back to the home screen. | Reply to an active buyer inquiry in the inbox.

9.3 Usability Metrics & Targets
To validate design success, evaluation results must meet the following thresholds:
1. Task Completion Success Rate (TCR): >= 90% of participants must complete the tasks without assistance.
2. Mean Error Rate (MER): Users must average < 2.0 input or navigation errors per task.
3. Subjective Customer Satisfaction (CSAT): Likert questionnaire ease-of-use scores must average >= 85%.
4. System Usability Scale (SUS): The platform must achieve a composite score >= 80 (classifying usability as "Good" to "Excellent").

9.4 System Usability Scale (SUS) Questionnaire
The standard 10-item Likert scale (1: Strongly Disagree to 5: Strongly Agree) will be completed post-testing. The score is calculated as:
Score = ( Sum (Odd Items - 1) + Sum (5 - Even Items) ) * 2.5

10. System Limitations, Risks & Mitigations

10.1 Current System Limitations
* Network Dependability: High-resolution property images require fast connections. On network speeds below 4G, card rendering can slow down, increasing page load times.
* Device Alignment: Differences in mobile webview rendering can cause minor layout shifts in horizontal navigation headers on older devices.

10.2 Identified Risks & Mitigations
* Risk: Credit System Adoption: Mozambican sellers used to free social media posting may find a credit-based model confusing.  
  Mitigation: The Seller App features a clear token counter that displays the direct monetary value (e.g. 500 MT = 50 credits), removing mental arithmetic.
* Risk: Payment Fraud: Fraudulent agents could exploit listing creation.  
  Mitigation: The Seller App onboarding requires identity verification via the biometric scanner wizard, awarding "Verified" badges to legitimate sellers.

11. Conclusion
The Nopin real estate ecosystem demonstrates that moving away from pin-heavy map interfaces in favor of a clean, card-driven design significantly lowers cognitive load for real estate users in Mozambique. By separating the platform into dedicated Buyer and Seller apps, the design respects the distinct mental models of each group, streamlining tasks and eliminating unnecessary interaction friction. 

Our integration of real-world tools — including an M-Pesa ecosystem STK Push, Supabase visitor tracking, and EmailJS receipt sync — demonstrates that UCD can produce a system that is usable, secure, and ready for deployment.
